## How to deliver this (the teaching model — same as Day 1)

**1. Active, not lecture-only — "I do → we do → you do."** Adults learn by doing. On every block you explain the idea, you walk one real example together (the deck has real screenshots and clips — Ahrefs KD, wisor.ai's E-E-A-T page, the Kling before/after, the live AI Overview, HeyGen and Arcads), and then they apply it to their own business. The free-tools slide and the two hands-on sessions are the "you do" — that's the point of the day.

**2. Concrete before abstract — ask before you tell.** Open each block with something they recognise ("your website traffic dropped this year", "which of these two clips is the AI one?"), throw the question to the room, let two or three answers land, and only then put the framework on screen. The example earns the theory; never the reverse.

**3. One running spine.** Bring every slide back to the same four-beat picture: **search is splitting (SEO + AEO) → AI collapses the cost of producing marketing → so you scale your judgement, not your headcount → and you guard the brand while you do it.** By the ethics session they should be able to place every tool and tactic on that spine.

**4. Click the live elements.** The video cards, the tool logos, and the AI-Overview screenshot are all clickable. Open one or two of them live so the room sees the real thing, not a description of it.

**5. Pacing (full day):** Part 1 + Session 1 (~75 min) → break → Session 2 video (~75 min) → lunch → Session 3 hands-on (~90 min) → Channels/Paid (~30 min) → Session 5 agentic (~90 min) → break → Session 4 ethics (~30 min) → Q&A + quiz + close. For a shorter run, trim the prompt-anatomy depth in Session 2 and the building-block slides in Session 5; keep the hooks and the worked examples.

# PART ONE — AI-NATIVE MARKETING

### DIVIDER — AI-Native Marketing
**Say (the thesis — let it land):** "Here's the one idea the whole morning hangs on. AI collapses the *cost* of producing marketing. Most people hear that as a cost-saving story — fire the agency, save money. That's the small prize. ### [02] Before AI
**Say:** "Picture how marketing used to run. Plan, then campaign, then a long wait to see if it worked — and a human did every single step: pull the data, write the copy, make the image, publish it, read the results. ### [03] With AI
**Say:** "Now look what AI takes over — the *doing*. It chews through more data than a person can, drafts the campaign, publishes at scale, and iterates without getting tired. So the bottleneck moves. It's no longer hands and hours; it's your judgement — what's worth making, what's on-brand, where you point the thing. AI does the doing; you decide what's worth doing. ### [04] Why does this matter for SMEs?
**Say:** "Walk it through for a small business. Testing gets faster, so every bet carries less risk. More iterations means you learn quicker, which means you find your real customers sooner. ### [05] AI didn't change *what* good marketing is
**Say (let it land, then pause):** "Notice what AI did *not* touch. It didn't change what good marketing is — the positioning, the audience, the offer from yesterday all still decide who wins. What it changed is the *speed* you can do it at. Same game, faster loop." Pause, then turn to search.

# SESSION 1 — SEO & GEO (AEO)

### DIVIDER — Session 1: SEO & GEO
**Say:** "Search is quietly splitting into two jobs. One is ranking on Google the way you always have — that's SEO. The other is getting *referenced by the AI* when it writes the answer for someone — that's GEO, or AEO. A year ago you needed one of these. Now you need both, and most of your competitors are only doing the first."

### [06] The terms: GEO & AEO
**Say:** "Two acronyms, one idea — don't let the jargon scare anyone. GEO is Generative Engine Optimisation, AEO is Answer Engine Optimisation. In practice they're the same move: optimise so the AI *cites you* in its answer, not just so Google ranks your blue link."

### [07] SEO isn't dying
**Say (ask first):** "Quick — hands up, who's been told SEO is dead? (a few hands) Let's kill that myth right here. Look at the screen — *this* is still SEO, the classic ten blue links, and they're not going anywhere. GEO doesn't replace SEO; it sits *on top* of it. The good SEO work is exactly what makes you eligible to be cited by the AI in the first place." (Real Google SERP screenshot.)

### [08] SEO 101: Keywords
**Say:** "Keywords come in three tiers, and the whole game is one trade-off — how hard a word is to win versus how winnable it is for *you*. Real Ahrefs numbers on screen: 'digital marketing' scores 66 on difficulty with ninety thousand searches a month — beautiful, and you will never rank for it. Now look at 'digital marketing tips for small business' — difficulty 16, a hundred and forty searches, far less competition and far clearer intent. For an SME, that long-tail is where you actually show up. Smaller pond, real fish."

### [09] SEO 101: E-E-A-T
**Say:** "Google doesn't only rank pages — it judges whether to *trust* them, through something called E-E-A-T. Four letters: Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness. Say each in one plain line as you point — has the writer actually done the thing, do they know the field, does anyone else vouch for them, and can the reader trust what's on the page."

### [10] E-E-A-T in a real article
**Say:** "Here's E-E-A-T in the wild so it isn't theory — wisor.ai's freight-tools listicle. Experience is the named author with a real background. Expertise is the structured comparison table. Authoritativeness is the fact that it cites Freightos, Project44, Flexport. Trustworthiness is the date, the 'reviewed by' line, the compliance badges. Point at each one. This is what 'proving it' actually looks like on a page."

### [12] SEO 101: Structure
**Say:** "Brilliant content still won't rank if the engine can't *find, understand and trust* it — so structure is the plumbing underneath. Three things hold it up. Can they reach the page at all — that's crawlability. Is it fast, mobile-friendly, secure — that's the technical hygiene. And do the links line up — your internal links tell Google what matters on your own site, external links are other people vouching for you. Plumbing isn't glamorous, but a blocked pipe sinks the whole thing."

### [13] Your website traffic likely dropped this year
**Say (hook — ask first):** "Gut-check, hands up — whose website traffic is *down* this year? (wait for the hands) Look around, you're not alone. Here's the culprit: AI Overviews are quietly absorbing the click that used to land on your site. People get their answer up top and never come down." Sets up the AEO case.

### [14] AEO / GEO evolution
**Say:** "So success has to be redefined. It used to be 'we're number one on Google.' In this new world, winning is being the source the AI *reaches for* when it builds the answer. The old goals were rank, clicks, traffic. The new goals are: get cited, get summarised, shape the answer *before* anyone clicks anything, and be a source the model trusts."

### [15] The shift: mindset → operation → measurement
**Say:** "It's really one shift in how you think. The SEO mindset was about winning the *ranking* — chase keywords, pump out pages, count the traffic. The GEO mindset is about winning the *citation* — be clear, be the densest, most useful explanation of a concept, and get yourself included in the answer. You're no longer writing for a rank; you're writing to be quoted."

### [16] A real Google AI Overview
**Say:** "This is the whole thing in one screenshot — a live AI Overview for 'best SME AI implementation consultant in Singapore'. Watch what happens: the answer is assembled and the sources are cited *before* anyone clicks a result, and a handful of named businesses soak up all the visibility. So the only question that matters for you is — are you one of the names in that box, or not?"

### [17] Consumers are shifting outside Google too
**Say:** "And it isn't only Google. People are starting their search *inside* the chatbots now — AI search usage doubled year on year, that's the Wix number. Which means you're not just optimising for Google any more; you're optimising for what ChatGPT and Perplexity say about you."

### [18] Why AI SEO matters for your business
**Say:** "Now the counter-intuitive part. Yes, AI sends you fewer clicks. But the clicks it *does* send convert about four-and-a-half times better than ordinary search. Why? They arrive already convinced — the visitor trusts the answer that recommended you, so half your selling is done before they land. Fewer visitors, much warmer ones."

### [19] Clicks drop, decisions don't
**Say:** "And even when the click never happens, the *decision* often still does. Over eighty percent of people say the AI Overview helps them finalise a purchase. Read that carefully — the AI summary isn't just information, it's the moment they make up their mind. Which means being cited can win you the sale even when it sends you zero traffic. You influenced the decision without ever seeing the visitor."

### [20] Classic SEO vs AEO
**Say:** "Same goal — get found — but a different playbook, so let's walk the comparison together. The number you chase moves from rank-and-traffic to answer-rate and assisted conversions. You hold a lot *less* direct control over the outcome. You can now show up across ChatGPT, Perplexity, even Reddit and Wikipedia, not just the Google results page. And the work shifts toward things engines can read cleanly — FAQ schema, clear entities, machine-readable context — plus building your reputation *off* your own site, where the models are reading."

### [21] Tools
**Say:** "Three jobs, a tool for each — don't memorise logos, just know what each *job* is. For classic SEO, Semrush. For the new GEO question — 'do the AI engines actually cite me?' — that's Profound, Peec AI, or WorkDuo. And to see your own traffic, plain old Google Analytics. Here's the genuinely useful bit you can do tomorrow: in GA4, go Reports, then Acquisition, then Traffic acquisition, and filter the session source for chatgpt.com, perplexity.ai, gemini, copilot, claude.ai — that's how you find the visitors the AI is already sending you." (Logos are clickable.)

# SESSION 2 — AI VIDEO GENERATION

The originality still has to come from you."

### [26] Real or fake?
**Say (interactive — ask first):** "Two clips coming up. Don't shout — just decide which one is the AI. (play both, take a show of hands, then reveal: one's real, one is fully generated.) ### [27] Image → Video
**Say:** "Three ways to generate, one per slide, and this is the first. Image-to-video: you hand it a reference picture and the AI adds the motion — look at the Kling before-and-after, same woman, now walking with a dog. The tools here are Kling AI and Higgsfield. Reach for it when consistency matters — products, characters, fashion, anything that has to stay *itself* across the shot. The flow is simple: upload the image, give it a short motion prompt, generate a few takes, keep the best one."

### [28] Text → Video
**Say:** "Second way — text-to-video. No reference at all, pure prompt; the model invents the whole frame. Tools are Sora, Runway, Kling. This is your ideation gear, best for concepts you're still dreaming up. You write the full seven-layer prompt, generate, and iterate. It's the most flexible of the three and the hardest to keep on a leash." (Watch-example link.)

### [29] Video → Video
**Say:** "Third way — video-to-video. You feed it footage you already shot and it restyles or transforms it. Runway's the tool. Best when you want a fresh look or a variation on something real, and the catch is that you're always boxed in by the original clip." (Watch-example link.)

### [30] How to generate killer scripts
**Say:** "Before any visuals, the words. The trick isn't to write from a blank page — it's to find a video that's already winning in your category, transcribe it, take it apart, and have ChatGPT adapt it to you. A good short script runs on six beats and each does exactly one job: you open on a hook to stop the scroll, drop into a relatable moment so they see themselves, hit a shift where something turns, slide the product in softly, pay it off casually, and end on a light call to action. Read the GRWM example one beat at a time so they hear the rhythm."

### [31] Writing a scroll-stopping hook
**Say:** "The first two seconds decide whether anyone sees the rest. A strong hook does three things — it's specific, it creates a little tension, and it talks to *one* person, not a crowd. There are about six flavours you can pull from: open with a question, make a contrarian claim, confess something, take a point of view, drop a shocking stat, or open a curiosity gap. The test is simple — a weak hook *describes*; a strong hook makes someone *react*."

### [32] AI script example
**Say:** "Here's the full GRWM script on screen, and you can see the structure doing its work — hook, relatable, shift, soft product, payoff, all in order. This is exactly the kind of thing you'd hand to an avatar tool or a voice model to perform."

### [33] How to generate a good video prompt
**Say:** "Now the visual brief, and this is the framework to actually remember today — seven layers. You're specifying the object, the physics, the motion, the camera, the environment, the style, and the negatives — the things you *don't* want. You can fill all seven by hand for full control, or take the shortcut: drop the seven headings into ChatGPT and let it write the finished prompt for you. Either you direct each layer, or the model guesses — and it guesses generic."

### [34] One full prompt — all seven layers
**Say:** "This is what the assembled prompt looks like once the seven layers are stitched together — the delivery-robot example, all of it in one paragraph. Point out the tail end especially: the negative constraints, 'no flicker, no added text.' That's where most people forget to steer, and it's where the weird artefacts come from."

# CHANNELS & PAID MARKETING

### [38] Marketing channels overview
**Say:** "So where does all this AI-made content actually go to work? Everywhere — email, organic posts in text and image, organic video, paid ads, and the blog for SEO and GEO. That's the quiet superpower: one idea, made once, can now show up across every channel almost instantly."

### [39] Paid marketing
**Say:** "Two different jobs, don't confuse them. Organic video is for *discovery* — top of funnel, building trust on TikTok, Reels, Shorts; hook them in the first two or three seconds, and authenticity beats polish every time. Paid video is for *distribution* — you take what's already working and pour fuel on it, retarget, test angles cheaply. What AI changes here is the *speed* of iteration. What it doesn't change is the boring stuff that still wins: good targeting, budget discipline, a clear message."

### [40] Before you run ads: four questions
**Say (ask first):** "Before anyone spends a dollar, you have to answer four questions — and I'll ask the room. What's the goal? Who's the audience, and are they cold, warm, or hot? What message are we actually testing? And what does success look like? That last one is the killer: if you can't define success up front, you literally cannot measure it, and you're just burning money with a nice dashboard."

### [41] Measurement
**Say:** "Know which number you're chasing *before* you boost anything. On organic, the honest signals are retention — did they get past the first few seconds — plus saves, shares, and comments; raw view counts will lie to you. On paid, it's the cost letters — cost per click, cost per lead, cost to acquire a customer, and return on ad spend. Pick your one number first, then optimise toward it."

**Say:** "Cleanest way to hold the difference: a skill is a recipe card, an agent is a specialist chef. One is a repeatable procedure you follow; the other is a role with judgement and tools of its own. Knowing which one a problem needs is most of the game."

### {S5} Breakout — build a skill or agent
**Facilitate (you-do):** "Teams of three, thirty minutes. Build a skill *or* an agent that solves one real task from your actual desk. The bar isn't a working tool — it's a sharp description plus one paragraph of body. Get the thinking right; the wiring is easy."

### {S5} Stuck? Start with this prompt
**Say:** "If you freeze at the blank page, paste exactly this: 'I want to do X. Ask me clarifying questions. Don't execute until I say OK.' That one line flips Claude from guessing to interviewing you — make it ask before it acts."

### {S5-65} Break (10 min)
**Say:** "Ten minutes. The section right after the break — context — is honestly the most important one of the whole session, so come back sharp."

### {S5-66} Show & tell
**Facilitate:** "One team at a time, two minutes each — what it does in sixty seconds, when to use it in thirty, who runs it in thirty. We give live feedback on the spot: is the description sharp, is the scope right, are the tools the right ones, does the first line of the body actually do work."

### {S5} The context window — what it is
**Say:** "Almost every frustration you'll ever have with Claude traces back to one idea, so we're going to nail it. The context window is simply the total amount of text Claude can hold in mind while it answers you. Before skills and agents make sense, you need this picture in your head."

### {S5} Signs it's congested
**Say:** "You'll know the window's overloaded by the symptoms. It forgets an instruction you gave twenty turns ago. The answers get shorter and blander. It quietly drops a step. And the instinct — pile on *more* instructions — makes it worse. The fix is to offload or reset, not to shout louder."

### {S5} Everything flows into one brain
**Say:** "Picture all of it pouring into one container — your CLAUDE.md, the project files, the memory file, every skill, every connector's tool list. They all land in the same working memory and share one ceiling. Nothing gets its own private shelf."

### {S5} What counts toward your window
**Say:** "And it all has a price. Everything Claude *reads* or *writes* costs space — your instructions file, every skill and agent description, the connector tool lists. So budget it like you'd budget a suitcase; you can't pack everything."

### {S5} The real problem — context rot
**Say:** "Here's the counter-intuitive bit that catches everyone. Bigger is *not* better. Accuracy actually decays as the window fills up, even when everything technically still fits. You prevent it the same handful of ways: trim the instructions file, break the work into skills, save things to files instead of holding them in your head, run /compact, and reset when it's tired."

### {S5-72} Hallucinated report → rebuilt the skill
**Say:** "Let me close with a real post-mortem. We had one giant do-everything skill, and it shipped a report full of invented numbers. We didn't patch it with more rules — we *rebuilt* it as smaller pieces. That's the whole lesson of this section: the cure for context rot is better architecture, not more instructions."

# SESSION 4 — TRENDS, ETHICS & IP *(the ending session)*

### DIVIDER — Tea Break
**Say:** "One last stretch after this — and it's the part that protects your brand."

### DIVIDER — Session 4
**Say:** "The fast, fun part is easy — you've done it all day. *This* is the part that protects your brand, your customers, and your reputation. Don't tune out for the landing."

### [73] Six places AI marketing can go wrong
**Say:** "Six ways this can bite you, and we'll take them one slide at a time — data privacy, the IP of what you feed in, disclosure and trust, the integrity of what comes out, model bias, and over-automation. None of them are reasons to stop; all of them are reasons to look before you leap."

### [74] 1 · Data privacy & sensitive information
**Say:** "First rule, and it's a hard line — never paste in personal data, raw customer records, financials, contracts, or anything regulated. Here's the trap that catches smart people: the AI rewards you for context, so the *more* you feed it the better the answer feels — and you talk yourself into oversharing. Most of these tools are not your private, confidential advisor. Treat the chat box like a postcard, not a diary."

### [75] 2 · Intellectual property of inputs
**Say:** "Uploading an image or a prompt does *not* hand you ownership of what comes out. So always ask two things before you use it commercially — do I actually own the rights to what I put *in*, and who owns the output, and am I even allowed to sell with it? And steer clear of mimicking a real person or training on a competitor's assets; that's where the lawsuits live."

### [76] 3 · Disclosure & trust
**Say:** "Audiences care about what's real — especially with testimonials and UGC, the exact formats we just learned to fake. The risk is passing AI off as a human, inventing 'real people' who don't exist. So disclose where it matters, and hold the line: use AI as *augmentation*, not *impersonation*."

### [77] 4 · Integrity of output
**Say:** "AI is fluent, confident, and *sometimes flat wrong*. And the real danger isn't only the bad fact — it's the credibility you lose when someone catches it. Watch for unverified claims, invented statistics, capabilities you can't back up, and that flat generic 'AI tone'. A human checks every output for accuracy, for tone, for cultural fit before it ships."

### [78] 5 · Model bias & brand risk
**Say:** "Models learn from human data, so they inherit human bias — they'll stereotype, exclude, or misrepresent without meaning to. For a small business that shows up as a tone that's slightly off, a cultural blind spot, an unintended offence. It bites hardest on global, social-impact, or inclusion messaging — exactly where it matters most. So get more than one set of eyes, ideally diverse ones, on anything sensitive."

### [79] 6 · Over-automation & brand dilution
**Say:** "The last one is the seductive one, because AI makes it so *easy* to publish more, faster, everywhere — and volume is not the same thing as a brand. What goes wrong is a voice that wanders, messaging that flattens into generic, that 'AI sameness', and an audience that simply gets tired of you. The guard is to decide your brand voice and your content boundaries *up front*, before the firehose is on."

### [80] Forward-looking trends
**Say:** "Three things to keep an eye on as you leave. One — search keeps shifting toward answers, so you'll have more influence even with less traffic. Two — iteration becomes the real edge; the winners test faster, they don't spend more. And three — the consultant's job becomes being a *filter*; when anyone can run the tools, judgement is the thing people pay for."

### [81] What changes, what doesn't
**Say (the close of content — say it slowly):** "Here's the whole day in two lists. What AI changes: the speed, the cost, the scale. What it does *not* change: who's accountable, whether people trust you, whether you act ethically. The business fundamentals still decide who wins. If you remember one sentence from today, that's the one."
